Livelihoods

Food security for vulnerable people of Northern Uganda (FOSPU)

CPF successfully implemented a food security program in Amuru district; Food Security for Vulnerable People of Northern Uganda (FOSPU) Project funded by ACDI/VOCA/ USAID targeting 1,500 households and 3000 small holders farmers who were supported to improve household productivity of Rice, Groundnut, Beans and cassava. This was done through provision of training in the following key technical areas to the project beneficiaries:

Agronomy Technical Area: Beneficiaries were trained on good agricultural practices such as early land preparation, early planting, seeds selection, correct spacing, pests and disease control and Demonstration farms were established to pass on knowledge to the farmer groups. One hundred demonstration plots were established, one in each of the groups.

Farming as a business (FaaB) Technical Area: CPF trained the beneficiary groups in basic skills such as enterprise selection, record keeping, business management skills, and risk management. Each of the 100 groups received a training of between 3 to 7 sessions in this section.

Post-harvest handling and marketing technical Area: CPF trained 3000 beneficiaries in Post-harvest handling with respect to the target crops (Cassava, Rice, Beans and groundnuts). These 30 FEWs will in turn train the rest of the beneficiary farmers. Three hundred Demonstrations storage facilities (granaries) will be built, one in each group. These improved granaries will be constructed at homes of selected contact farmers as a way of encouraging others to replicate the practice in their households. The practical demonstrations of the construction of improved granaries will be done towards the beginning of the harvesting season.

Self-help Groups development Technical Area: The project organizeD communities to work together in groups, access production inputs as groups and learn to address issues affecting them collectively. CPF trained the 100 groups in savings mobilization, loans management, marketing as well other group dynamics. The groups were guided to agree on an amount of money that will be contributed by each member on a weekly basis to establish group savings. These served as revolving funds from where members accessed small loans to finance their activities. The households that benefited from the project later came together and formed Lamogi Farmers’ Cooperative Society Ltd.

Girl-child Education Support Project (GESP)

The Girl-child Education Support Project (GESP) is being implemented in Amuru district with support from KNH. The project was initiated on the tenet that there still remains a big issue of big gender gap in enrolment of girls compared to that of boys in primary schools in Amuru district and northern Uganda in general.The project is therefore meant to reduce on this gender gap and increase girl child enrolment in primary schools. It equally focuses on assisting girls who are ‘at risk’ of dropping out, to remain in school while encouraging those who have dropped out to resume education. These gaps are attributed to the two decades’ conflict that confine the population into internally displaced camps rendering parents helpless in meeting the basic school needs for their children. As communities resettle back to their original homes, schools are far from homes, sanitation and hygiene facilities in schools are inadequate and poorly maintained.

The project therefore aim at improving the quality of life and education of 3000 children in Amuru district by 2015 and this would be achieved through 2 objectives namely; (i) improving enrolment and retention of 1800 girl children (ii) increasing child rights awareness of 3000 children on issues that affect them in schools and communities.A total of 3299 girls and 3309 boys are benefiting from the project through children clubs, constructed latrines with changing rooms, participation in designated activities such as essay writing competition, debates, development of IEC materials, participation in Day of African Child and International Women’s day, poem citations, dances/songs and drama performances and friendly football and netball matches between children clubs members.

An estimated 4500 children have also been reached through radio talk shows and sport jingles, out of school outreaches, essay competition, community sensitization meetings with parents on their roles and roles of stake holders in education of children.

SHONGLAP Project

Shonglap means a dialogue held between the Animators and the adolescent girls in the centers among the girls in order to ensure development of their living standards, mentally and economically.

It started from Bangladesh in India where it was realized that the young girls do not know much about life and how to live it. The main reasons for this are: they don’t have self-confidence, and they do not know the different issues about their day to day lives. The most important issues among these are; little knowledge about their rights as human beings and also people in the community and do not know how to stay healthy and what food is good for health. On the other hand, there are few chances of them getting work to earn money. Shonglap was introduced to remove all those and develop the standard of living of the Adolescent girls.

This project is working towards the development of the standard of living of the adolescent girls through increasing awareness in life related issues, acquiring of knowledge/ literacy, professional life skills with the view of making the young girls independent. It will be achieved through the Shonglap centers established for the young adolescent girls in the project areas.

CBEI Project

Community based education initiative (CBEI) is a stromme foundation funded project in partnership with CPF in Lamogi sub countyAmuru District. CBEI project aims at improving the level and quality of education of the beneficiaries in the selected government schools. In this year 2012, four schools were selected,ieTekibur primary school, Pagak primary school,Olwalmucaja primary school and Agwayugi primary school in Amuru district. The project aims at reducing the drop-out rates in the four schools,improving the level of performance in the primary leaving exams and improving school infrastructure e.g. classrooms, latrines, teachers’ houses built and changing rooms for girls and many others contributions like provision of hygiene facilities like slashers, hoes, wheelbarrows etc. The overall issue or challenge addressedby proposed intervention is reducing dropout rates and improving performance of pupils in public primary schools in Amuru District by 2016.

Community Action Against Sexual and Gender Based Violence.

Charity for Peace Foundation with funding of 288,665,360/= from CARE INERNATIONAL implemented a two years’ project with the goal of building Institutional and Community Mechanisms to Prevent and respond to Sexual and Gender Based Violence in conflict and post conflict situation. The project had the component of male involvement campaign where men were encouraged to act as role models in the community by supporting their women in all aspects of life.

This project was implemented in six camps within two districts of Amuru and Nwoya. The project started in August 2007 and ended in March 2010.

Youth Education Programme (YEP)

This Programme contributed to the process of long-lasting empowerment of children and youth residing within the Internally Displaced Persons (IDP) camps through educational, recreational, health, psychosocial, income generating activities and vocational activities allowing them to become powerful instruments of knowledge and development in their local communities and beyond.

With a meager fund of $15,000 from St Alban Episcopal church Washington DC, Charity for Peace Foundation operated YEP in two Camps of COOPE and TE-TUGU. The Programme ran with 100 youths in each camp who were basically formerly abducted, child mothers and those vulnerable youths who lost their normal truck of education due to the war and poverty.The youths were later supported in projects such as Bakery, Poultry, ox-plough, Piggery, beads making and modern agriculture to be financed for them. This funding ended in December 2006.

Children At Home (CAH) Night Commuter's Centre

Charity for Peace Foundation opened a night commuter’s center in Gulu in July 2003 with 7328 children. The number kept on reducing and because of the Peace process in Juba, Southern Sudan, and the number has reduced to 37 children. These children were still commuting for various reasons ranging from child headed families, total orphans, street children, lack of accommodation etc.

With emergency one time funds from UNICEF and Save the Children, we were able to construct 4 halls as dormitory for the children. We also constructed office block where we are operating now.

In the year 2007, Action for Street Kids (ASK), UK came in and help us to start survey and follow up of children and the families they come from. We also managed to put the Care Givers of the children in a group of bead making as part of income generating activity. Mr. Peter Tyrer( African Children Fund, UK) visited Uganda in the Month of August and after analyzing the problem of the children, we agreed that the center will become re-habilitation centre for children. It will be called Temporal Residential Care for Orphans and Vulnerable Children of Northern Uganda(TRC). This center is now operational and helps to re-habilitate and reintegrate orphans, street children and other vulnerable children due to the war in northern Uganda. We re-integrate them in the community by empowering Care Givers and Mentors of these children in the community.

Community Action against Child Trafficking In Northern Uganda.

This project provided information to community, key community leaders and resource persons to reduce the rampant trafficking of children from the poor rural and especially war affected Northern Districts of Uganda to the perceived wealthier other Districts of Uganda and beyond.

Every year children from the poor northern Districts of Uganda are duped by people from the relatively richer parts of Uganda and beyond and taken as house helpers, baby sitters, sometimes as sex slaves (workers) and sometime in the name of paying school fees for them. Most often, these are children below 15 years of age, who due to their vulnerable circumstances miss several opportunities to develop themselves and to live with their relatives and other support systems.

Funded by National Democratic Institute for International Affairs (NDI), the 4 months project at $4000 enlisted the support of community and community leaders to sensitize the community against allowing their children to be trafficked and wasted. The project created community structure which is fighting child trafficking among the community

This project was just a beep in the community. When awareness was raised, community reported cases which needed action but funding has never been raised to continue with sensitization and action to support victims of child trafficking.
